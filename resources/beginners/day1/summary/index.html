    üó£Ô∏è The Sentence

    !!! note "Overview" Language is thought expressed in words.
    To express thought, words are combined into sentences.
    A sentence is a group of words which expresses a complete thought.

    ‚ú≥Ô∏è Types of Sentences

    ‚Ä¢ Declarative ‚Äî asserts something as a fact.
    ‚Ä¢ Interrogative ‚Äî asks a question.
    ‚Ä¢ Imperative ‚Äî expresses a command or request.
    ‚Ä¢ Exclamatory ‚Äî expresses an emotion, such as surprise or grief, in the form of an exclamation or cry.

    A declarative, interrogative, or imperative sentence may also be exclamatory.

    üî§ Subject and Predicate

    !!! tip "Structure of a Sentence" ‚Ä¢ Every sentence consists of a subject and a predicate.

    ‚Ä¢ The subject of a sentence designates the person, place or thing that the sentence is about;
    the predicate is that which is said of the subject.

    ‚Ä¢ The simple subject of a sentence is a noun or pronoun.
    The simple predicate of a sentence is a verb or verb phrase.

    ‚Ä¢ The simple subject is the main noun or pronoun that the sentence is about.
    When combined with all the other words that explain, modify or complete its meaning, it forms the complete subject.

    ‚Ä¢ The simple predicate is the main verb or verb phrase that asserts something about the subject.
    When combined with all the other words that explain, modify or complete its meaning, it forms the complete predicate.

    ‚Ä¢ A compound subject or predicate consists of two or more simple subjects or predicates, joined, when necessary, by conjunctions.

    ‚Ä¢ Either the subject or the predicate, or both, may be compound.

    üß± The Parts of Speech

    In accordance with their use in a sentence, words are divided into eight parts of speech:
    nouns, pronouns, adjectives, verbs, adverbs, prepositions, conjunctions, and interjections.

    1Ô∏è‚É£ Nouns and Pronouns

    (1) A noun is the name of a person, place or thing.

    (2) A pronoun is a word used instead of a noun.
    It designates a person, place or thing without naming it.

    Nouns and pronouns are called substantives.
    The substantive to which a pronoun refers is called its antecedent.

    2Ô∏è‚É£ Adjectives

    (3) An adjective is a word that describes or limits a substantive.
    This is usually done by indicating a specific quality.

    An adjective is said to ‚Äúbelong‚Äù to the substantive that it describes or limits.

    An adjective that describes is called a descriptive adjective;
    one that points out or designates is called a definitive adjective.

    An article is considered an adjective.
    ‚ÄòA‚Äô or ‚Äòan‚Äô are indefinite articles used to denote a nonspecific substantive.
    ‚ÄòThe‚Äô is a definite article used to denote a specific substantive.

    3Ô∏è‚É£ Verbs

    (4) A verb is a word that asserts something (usually an action) concerning a person, place or thing.

    Some verbs express a state or condition rather than an action.
    A group of words that is used as a verb is called a verb phrase.

    Certain verbs, when used to make verb-phrases, are called auxiliary (or aiding) verbs,
    because they help other verbs to express an action or a state.

    Is (in its various forms) and several other verbs may be used to frame sentences in which
    a word or words in the predicate describe or define the subject.
    In such sentences, is and other verbs used for the same purpose are called copulative (or linking) verbs.

    The infinitive is a verb form that can act as a noun or a modifier and is commonly preceded by the preposition to,
    which is indicative of the infinitive form.

    The participle is a verb form that has no subject and acts like an adjective.
    It expresses an action or a state in such a way as to describe or limit a substantive.

    A participle is said to ‚Äúbelong‚Äù to the substantive that it describes or limits.

    The two main types of participles are present participles and past participles, based on the time that they denote.

    4Ô∏è‚É£ Adverbs

    (5) An adverb is a word that modifies a verb, an adjective, or another adverb.

    A word or group of words that changes or modifies the meaning of another word is called a modifier.
    Adjectives and adverbs are both modifiers.

    5Ô∏è‚É£ Prepositions, Conjunctions, and Interjections

    (6) A preposition is a word placed before a substantive to show its relation to another word in the sentence.
    The substantive that follows a preposition is called its object.

    (7) A conjunction connects words or groups of words.

    (8) An interjection is a cry or other exclamatory sound expressing an emotion,
    such as surprise, anger or pleasure.

    üßæ Additional Notes

    ‚Ä¢ The meaning of a word in a sentence determines the part of speech to which it belongs.
    ‚Ä¢ The same word may sometimes be one part of speech, and sometimes another, particularly with infinitives and participles.
    ‚Ä¢ Inflection is a change in the form of a word indicating a difference in its meaning.
    A word thus changed in form is said to be inflected.
    ‚Ä¢ The inflection of a substantive is called its declension;
    that of an adjective or an adverb its comparison;
    that of a verb its conjugation.

    üß© Phrases

    !!! abstract "Definition" A phrase is a group of connected words that does not contain a subject or a predicate.

    A phrase is equivalent to a part of speech:

    ‚Ä¢ A phrase used as a noun is called a noun phrase.
    ‚Ä¢ A phrase used as a verb is called a verb phrase.
    ‚Ä¢ A phrase used as an adjective is called an adjective phrase.
    ‚Ä¢ A phrase used as an adverb is called an adverbial phrase.

    Adjective or adverbial phrases consisting of a preposition and its object,
    with or without other words, are called prepositional phrases.

    üîó Clauses

    A clause is a group of words that forms part of a sentence and contains a subject and a predicate.

    A clause used as a part of speech is called a subordinate clause.
    All other clauses are called independent clauses.

    Clauses of the same order or rank are said to be coordinate clauses.

    üìò Types of Sentences

    ‚Ä¢ Sentences may be simple, compound, complex, or compound-complex.
    ‚Ä¢ A simple sentence has only one subject and one predicate, either or both of which may be compound.
    ‚Ä¢ A compound sentence consists of two or more independent coordinate clauses, which may or may not be joined by conjunctions.
    ‚Ä¢ A complex sentence consists of two or more clauses, one of which is independent and the rest subordinate.
    ‚Ä¢ A compound sentence in which one or more of the coordinate clauses are complex is called a compound-complex sentence.

    üß† Subordinate Clauses

    ‚Ä¢ Subordinate clauses, like phrases, are used as parts of speech.
    They serve as substitutes for nouns, adjectives, or adverbs.
    ‚Ä¢ A subordinate clause that is used as a noun is called a noun (or substantive) clause.
    ‚Ä¢ A subordinate clause that modifies a substantive is called an adjective clause.
    ‚Ä¢ A subordinate clause that serves as an adverbial modifier is called an adverbial clause.
