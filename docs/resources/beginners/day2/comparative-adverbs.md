# Comparative Adverbs

### üîπ What Are Comparative Adverbs?

One of the jobs of an [**adverb**](https://www.englishclub.com/grammar/adverbs.php){ target="_blank" } is to modify a **verb action**, for example:

> Joe ran **fast**.

If we want to compare one verb action with another, we can use a **comparative adverb**, for example:

> Joe ran fast, but Mary came first because she ran **faster**.

We use **comparative adverbs** when talking about **two actions** (not three or more).  
üëâ Comparison is always between **two things**.

## üß† How Do We Use Comparative Adverbs?

Now that you know how to **form** comparative adverbs,  
let‚Äôs see how to **use** them in sentences.

> Notice that we use **more** to show an *increase* in the action,  
> and **less** to show a *decrease*.  
> Also note that the comparative adverb is often followed by **than**.

**Examples:**

- Trains go fast, but planes go **faster**.  
- Planes go **faster than** trains.  
- Trains don‚Äôt go **faster than** planes.  
- Trains go **more slowly than** planes.  
- Planes go **less slowly than** trains.  
- Joe won because he played **better than** Jane.  
- Did cities grow **more quickly** after the Industrial Revolution?  
- He hit the ball **more powerfully than** his competitor.  
- As we get older, we remember things **less easily**.  
- Could you talk a bit **more quietly**, please?  
- Could you talk a bit **less loudly**, please?  
- I can‚Äôt hear you ‚Äî please speak **louder / more loudly**.

---

### üåç Comparing Groups

Although we use comparative adverbs when talking about **two actions**,  
one or both of those actions may represent a **group**.

> The planet **Mercury** revolves around the sun **faster than all the other planets**.

Here, we are comparing **one action (Mercury‚Äôs)** to **one other group of actions (all the other planets)**.

---

> ‚úÖ **Summary:**  
> Use *-er* for short adverbs,  
> *more/less* for long adverbs,  
> and remember the irregulars (*better, worse, farther*).
